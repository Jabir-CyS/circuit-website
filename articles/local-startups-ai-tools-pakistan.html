Local startups are quietly building Pakistan's next wave of AI tools

A handful of small teams in Lahore and Karachi are shipping AI products for local businesses — skipping the hype cycle and going straight for problems no one else is solving.

SK Sara Khalid · 2026-07-13 · 4 min read

While most of the attention in AI right now goes to large language models built by well-funded labs abroad, a quieter shift is happening closer to home. Small teams across Lahore and Karachi are building narrow, practical AI tools aimed at local business problems — inventory forecasting for retailers, Urdu-language customer support bots, and automated bookkeeping for small shops.

None of these products are trying to compete with the big foundation model providers. Instead, they wrap existing models with workflows built specifically for how local businesses actually operate — which, founders say, is where the real opportunity is.

"Nobody wants a chatbot. They want their end-of-day sales tallied automatically," said one founder building for retail shops in Lahore. That kind of focus, on unglamorous but recurring problems, is what's letting these teams find paying customers fast — often before they've raised any outside funding at all.

The trend also reflects a broader pattern: as AI infrastructure becomes cheaper and more accessible, the advantage shifts from who has the biggest model to who understands the problem best. For local teams with deep context on how small businesses in Pakistan actually run, that's a real edge.

It's still early. Most of these products serve a few dozen customers, not thousands. But the founders building them aren't chasing scale yet — they're chasing product-market fit in a market that's been mostly ignored by outside players.
