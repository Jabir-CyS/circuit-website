The quiet rise of browser-based phishing kits

Attackers are moving away from email and into the browser itself, using fake extensions to harvest logins in real time.

HR Hamza Rauf · 2026-07-08 · 4 min read

Email-based phishing is still common, but security researchers are increasingly flagging a different attack surface: fake browser extensions designed to look like legitimate productivity tools. Once installed, these extensions can quietly capture login credentials as they're typed, without ever tripping a spam filter.

Because extensions request broad permissions by default, users often don't notice anything unusual until credentials are already compromised. Unlike email phishing, there's no suspicious link to hover over — the extension already has access once it's installed.

Security teams recommend restricting extension installs to a vetted allow-list, reviewing permissions regularly, and treating any extension that requests access to "all websites" with real scrutiny — even ones that appear to have genuine reviews.
