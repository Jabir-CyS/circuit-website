We compared five budget phone cameras. One surprised us

You don't need to spend flagship money for a genuinely good camera anymore. Here's what actually stood out in daylight, low light, and portraits.

AF Ayesha Farooq · 2026-06-30 · 5 min read

Budget phones used to mean a clear compromise on camera quality. That gap has narrowed significantly, and in one case in this comparison, nearly closed entirely — a mid-range phone under a modest price point produced daylight photos that were difficult to distinguish from a phone costing nearly three times as much.

Low light remained the clearest differentiator across the group. Cheaper sensors still struggle to gather enough light without introducing visible noise, and software-based night modes only compensate so much before detail starts to smear.

Portrait mode edge detection — the software's ability to correctly blur the background around hair and fine detail — was the other major gap. The strongest budget option in this test handled it convincingly; two others struggled visibly around hair and glasses.
